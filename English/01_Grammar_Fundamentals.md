### ğŸš¨ TRAP ALERT: Common Pronoun Errors

**Trap 1: Between you and I** âŒ
- **Wrong**: Between you and I
- **Correct**: Between you and me
- **Why**: "Between" is a preposition; prepositions take object pronouns (me, not I)

**Trap 2: Him and me went** âŒ
- **Wrong**: Him and me went to the store
- **Correct**: He and I went to the store
- **Quick Test**: Remove the other person. "Me went" sounds wrong; "I went" is correct.

**Trap 3: Its vs It's**
- **It's** = It is / It has (contraction)
- **Its** = Belonging to it (possessive)
- **Why no apostrophe?** Possessive pronouns never use apostrophes (his, hers, ours, theirs, its)

**Trap 4: Who vs Whom**
```
WHO = Subject (he/she/they do something)
WHOM = Object (something is done to him/her/them)

Quick Test: Answer the question
"Who/Whom did you call?" â†’ I called HIM â†’ WHOM
"Who/Whom called you?" â†’ HE called â†’ WHO

## 1.4 Adjectives & Adverbs: The Modifiers

### Position Rules

**Adjectives**: Come BEFORE nouns (or after linking verbs)
```
A beautiful painting (before noun)
The painting is beautiful (after linking verb)
```

**Adverbs**: More flexible, but follow patterns
```
She spoke softly (after verb)
She quickly finished (before verb - for emphasis)
Fortunately, we arrived (start of sentence - for the whole sentence)
```

### ğŸš¨ TRAP ALERT: Adjective vs Adverb After Linking Verbs

**Question**: "She looks beautiful" or "She looks beautifully"?

**Answer**: Beautiful (adjective)

**WHY?** 
- "Looks" here is a LINKING verb (connecting her to a description)
- After linking verbs, use adjectives, not adverbs
- "She looks beautifully" would mean "her eyes perform the action of looking in a beautiful manner"

**Similar traps**:
- The food smells **good** âœ… (not "well" - unless you mean it has a working nose)
- I feel **bad** âœ… (not "badly" - unless your fingers can't sense properly)
- She seems **nervous** âœ… (not "nervously")

| Two+ syllables | Use more/most | beautiful â†’ more beautiful â†’ most beautiful |

# Part 2: Subject-Verb Agreement

> **The Golden Rule**: The verb must agree with its subject in number (singular/plural)

## The Core Principle

```
SINGULAR SUBJECT â†’ SINGULAR VERB
PLURAL SUBJECT â†’ PLURAL VERB

The dog runs. (singular + singular)
The dogs run. (plural + plural)
```

## ğŸ§  The "Ignore the Noise" Strategy

The key to mastering subject-verb agreement is identifying the TRUE subject by ignoring:
- Prepositional phrases
- Relative clauses
- Parenthetical expressions

```
"The box of chocolates IS on the table"
          â†‘ noise    â†‘ true subject is "box" (singular)
          
"The players, along with their coach, ARE practicing"
                    â†‘ noise           â†‘ Wait! "along with" doesn't add to subject
                                      
CORRECT: "The players, along with their coach, ARE practicing"
```

## ğŸš¨ TRAP ALERT: Tricky Subject-Verb Agreement Cases

### Trap 1: "Along with" / "As well as" / "Together with"

These DON'T make the subject plural:
```
The manager, along with the employees, WAS present. âœ…
(Subject is "manager" - singular)

Compare with:
The manager AND the employees WERE present. âœ…
(AND joins them into a plural subject)
```

### Trap 2: Either/Or, Neither/Nor

**Rule**: Verb agrees with the CLOSER subject

```
Neither the teacher nor the students WERE ready.
Neither the students nor the teacher WAS ready.
                              â†‘ closer subject
```

### Trap 3: Collective Nouns

```
UNIT = Singular verb
"The team IS performing well." (acting as one unit)

INDIVIDUALS = Plural verb
"The team ARE arguing among themselves." (acting separately)
```

### Trap 4: Indefinite Pronouns

**Always Singular**: each, either, neither, everyone, someone, anyone, no one, everybody, somebody, anybody, nobody, everything, something, anything, nothing

```
Everyone IS here. âœ…
Neither of the answers IS correct. âœ…
Each of the students HAS a book. âœ…
```

**Always Plural**: both, few, many, several

```
Many WERE absent. âœ…
Few HAVE understood. âœ…
```

**Depends on the noun**: some, any, all, most, none

```
Some of the water IS spilled. (water = uncountable = singular)
Some of the books ARE missing. (books = countable plural = plural)
```

### Trap 5: Numerical Expressions

```
SINGLE UNIT = Singular
"Ten kilometers IS a long distance."
"Two hours IS enough time."
"Five thousand rupees IS the fee."

INDIVIDUAL ITEMS = Plural  
"Ten people WERE injured."
```

### Trap 6: "The number" vs "A number"

```
THE number of students IS increasing. (specific quantity = singular)
A number of students HAVE passed. (many students = plural)
```

# Part 3: Articles & Determiners

## The Mental Model: Articles as Introduction Levels

```
A/AN = "Let me introduce someone new to you" (First mention, non-specific)
THE = "You already know who I'm talking about" (Specific, known)
Ã˜ (no article) = "We're speaking generally" (Abstract or plural general)

## A vs AN: The Sound Rule (Not Spelling!)

```
A + Consonant SOUND â†’ a university, a European, a one-rupee coin
AN + Vowel SOUND â†’ an hour, an honest man, an MBA, an X-ray
```

### ğŸš¨ Edge Cases

| Word | Article | Why |
|------|---------|-----|
| hour | AN hour | Silent 'h' â†’ starts with vowel sound 'ow' |
| honest | AN honest | Silent 'h' |
| university | A university | Starts with 'yu' consonant sound |
| uniform | A uniform | Starts with 'yu' consonant sound |
| European | A European | Starts with 'yu' consonant sound |
| one | A one | Starts with 'w' consonant sound |
| MBA | AN MBA | Starts with 'em' vowel sound |
| SBI | AN SBI | Starts with 'es' vowel sound |
| UFO | A UFO | Starts with 'yu' consonant sound |

## When to Use THE (Specific Reference)

### Use THE when:

1. **Second mention**: "I saw a dog. THE dog was brown."
2. **Unique things**: the sun, the moon, the earth, the world
3. **Superlatives**: the best, the tallest, the most beautiful
4. **Ordinals**: the first, the second, the last
5. **Known from context**: "Close the door." (the door in this room)
6. **Inventions/Species**: The computer changed the world. The lion is a wild animal.
7. **Names with Republic/Kingdom/States**: The USA, The UK, The Republic of India
8. **Mountain ranges/Oceans/Rivers/Deserts**: The Himalayas, The Pacific, The Ganges, The Sahara
9. **Newspapers**: The Times, The Hindu
10. **Musical instruments**: play the guitar, the piano

### DON'T Use THE with:

1. **Proper nouns** (names): India (not The India), Mumbai, Mount Everest
2. **Languages**: English, Hindi (not The English language - unless specifying)
3. **Meals**: breakfast, lunch, dinner
4. **Games/Sports**: cricket, football, chess
5. **Subjects**: Mathematics, Physics (but: the study of physics)
6. **Days/Months**: Monday, January
7. **Abstract nouns used generally**: Love is beautiful. Honesty is the best policy.
8. **Plural nouns used generally**: Dogs are loyal. Books are useful.

# Part 4: Sentence Structure

## The Five Basic Sentence Patterns

```
1. S + V
   "Birds fly."
   
2. S + V + O
   "She reads books."
   
3. S + V + C (Complement)
   "He is intelligent." (Linking verb + adjective/noun)
   
4. S + V + IO + DO (Two Objects)
   "She gave him a gift."
   (Indirect Object = receiver, Direct Object = thing given)
   
5. S + V + O + C (Object Complement)
   "They elected him president."
   (president describes the object "him")
```

## Sentence Types by Function

| Type | Purpose | Example | Punctuation |
|------|---------|---------|-------------|
| Declarative | Makes a statement | She is a doctor. | Period (.) |
| Interrogative | Asks a question | Is she a doctor? | Question mark (?) |
| Imperative | Gives a command | Be a good doctor. | Period or exclamation |
| Exclamatory | Expresses emotion | What a great doctor! | Exclamation mark (!) |

## Sentence Types by Structure

```
SIMPLE: One independent clause
â†’ "The sun rises in the east."

COMPOUND: Two+ independent clauses joined by FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so)
â†’ "The sun was shining, but it was cold."

COMPLEX: Independent clause + dependent clause(s)
â†’ "Although it was raining, we went out." 

COMPOUND-COMPLEX: 2+ independent clauses + 1+ dependent clause
â†’ "Although it was late, she worked hard, and she finished the project."
```
